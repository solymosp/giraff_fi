Moscow

First impressions
I arrived with a night train in the morning, the first obstacle I saw was a big Lenin monument inside the train station. I thought people had pulled most of them down, but they're really all over, absolutely ignored. Interesting thing was that you can't find a single Stalin statue, and I had my guesses why - in Hungary with the change all the monuments were cleared up, and streetnames got changed back to their originals too. Question was, how come that Lenin has survived all this in Russia? More interesting than this, it seemed to me after a while that some of the folks still tend to adore him for his deeds. As I heard later on, around 10-15 percents of the citizens vote still for the reformed communist party.

Watch them pictures!

The subways
Moscow is huge. With a population of 11 million, with all the gracious squares and monuments it's really one of the most impressive cities I have seen, the metro lines are richly decorated, and aren't put that deep underground as in St. Petersburg, where it was meant to serve as a nuclear bomb-shelter. I even had some sort of a home feeling as the wagons are just the same as in Budapest. ÷)

The subway decorations are typical soviet ones, representing the grace of the workers and the greatness of Lenin, and they are so dense that I had a slight feeling of being brainwashed even after a few days - for everyday commuters it could have been a true instrument of manipulation. As our Russian friend, Anton explained me later, they are absolutely overlooked today, the younger generations take the old symbols more for fashion than for signs of political affiliation. He knew the answer for my Stalin-dilemma too: he had been banned during the soviet regime yet by Hrushtshov, and it was the communist party that has cleared him up from the streets while giving Lenin an even greater praise.

Being a tourist in Moscow...
...is being lost among thousands of your kind in a place where some things just work differently. Though I myself come from a post-communist country, I have experienced plenty of behavioural schemes and regulations I never thought of. The weirdest thing is that in Russia people generally lack the sense for customer service, of course most of them never experienced it and therefore cannot be blamed, but for foreigners this might be a hard nut to crack. It happened to me not once that I had to go for the waiter in a restaurant, that receptionists behind the desk simply turned away when they didn't understand me, taxi drivers agressively try to persuade you into their cars, and wherever you have to queue up, count with at least 10 minutes per person - it REALLY never was faster than this. Officials may cross your way in the strangest situations too: my friend got seized by soldiers for taking a photo of a nice building, where the Ministry of Defence is seated. Though even these young privates were laughing about the whole story, they took him in front of some elder officer and he had to erase that picture - if you have a normal camera that means losing your pictures too, and if you happen not to speak Russian, well, that's your problem...

All in all, the city is really beautiful and definitely worth visiting, making your way through all this gives a nice taste of the way things used to work here some 10-15 years before.
