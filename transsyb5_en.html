Mongolia

Gettin' jiggy

Smugglers' paradise
From Ulan Ude we took a night-train straight to the Mongolian capital. We had been warned before that we were going to wait some 6 hours at the border, the reason why we figured out soon after boarding the train: an incredible traffic of goods is happening between the two countries, smugglers were busily trying to hide their things everywhere. The train was so full that the personnel had hard times finding some place for us, in the end we were sleeping in a couchette full of things, television, whatever you can imagine. There was a nice little girl in the cabin, she could talk some English too, so we got into a small-talk - she happened to say Mongolia was such a small country, and Miha and me broke out in laughter, trying to explain that mine can fit into Mongolia some 18 times, and Miha's at least 30... with China and Russia in the backyard she seemed to have an other impression about sizes. ÷)

Ulanbaatar
The border took just as long as said, the border guards practically dismantled the wagons and put them together again. In the morning finally we got to Ulanbaatar - certainly one of the most interesting cities I have seen so far, with people selling phonecalls on the street, gers (Mongolian tents) every here and there, and so undeveloped that there are absolutely no cashpoints all over the place. Mongolia has an area of 1.5 million square kilometers to a population of 2.6 million people - from this nearly one million live in the capital, the rest is dispersed throughout the country, most of them living a true nomad life herding animals. Banking - what for? ÷)

Finding a place to sleep isn't really a hassle, put on your backpack at the train station and you're going to be surrounded by hostel activists in 2 sec, offering nights for 3-4$, how cheap can you get? They have net connections, washing mashines, hot showers (this is a separate line under "facilities"! ÷), whatever you may need for having an everything's-alright-feeling.

Go for a jeeptour!

What else?
There are not too many possibilities to waste your time around in the country but to take a jeep-trip into the desert. Public transportation on the countryside is hopeless, if you want to get out of the capital you need a jeep and a driver who knows the ways - no paved roads, no traffic signs, use your instincts. ÷) Jeeptours are organized in a wide range from 2000$ per person down to 450$ a jeep for the same route. In the previous one you travel in a fancy recent model with your own cook and guide, but we took the cheap thing of course, an old Russian UAZ for 9 days with driver and petrol included. Pack your survival-kit and up we go!

1st day
We did the necessary shopping (loooads of water!) and drove out to the West. (I found some Hungarian Tibi cholcolate in the shop, "heee?") Stopped a few times, drank horsemilk (that's some weird stuff...), lost the good roads from under ourselves, went camping between some big rocky hills. Me and Mathieu climbed to the peaks, the view was really exceptional!

2nd day
This day we got to Karakorum, the former capital of the great Kans. Today its only spectacle is a buddhist monastery, indeed really nice, with some expensive souveniers you can buy there. We got some dried cheese from a nomad family on the way, one of them was quite tasty! In the evening we were camping on a true desert terrain, a great flat rocky place, with a wonderful clear sky, in the middle of nowhere - it was truly amazing.

3rd day
In the morning the van broke down, and our driver had to go for some spare parts so it looked like we have a couple of hours free. We went over to the next ger and gave some cookies to the family we found there, and tried to make up some sort of international communication, it's pretty funny to talk with body language for hours. ÷) A Holland guy had a discman with him, so we were showing every kinds of music to the children, and I made the mistake to give my watch into the hands of a local boy who came there on a horse - he checked it out and then when I turned my back to him he took it for a gift and rode away. Later he came back and took the discman for a gift too, but we got him trying to put it into his pants - I guess he thought the whole world is actually his. ÷) He gave my watch to his father, I saw him later on riding around with it. If you ever come to Mongolia, and have an expensive watch, don't give it out of your hands! (Mine was a cheap piece, luckily. ;-)

When being together with a Mongolian family, there are lots of rules and protocols you should consider, they originate in religious beliefs and superstitions mostly, and you may be seen rude or a bringer of bad luck if you don't know how to behave. Just to give an image, some of these rules are: when offering something, always do it with your right hand and always take it with your right; don't go counter-clockwise in a ger; cover your wrists in company; don't show your soles or back to the family altar, don't show your back to elder persons; if somebody offers food, always take it, you don't have to eat it if you don't like to, but take a bite at least; never give money to the parents, etc. Well, we were not exactly experts on this how-to-behave field, so after a while they began to look somewhat strange at us, but the driver came soon back so we had an excuse to leave and not bother them anymore. ÷)

4th day
This day was a real desert-day, 40 degrees centigrade, driving a lot to catch up with yesterday's delay. Saw the first camels on the way, and took a rest to visit the ruins of two majestic buddhist monasteries. In the evening we camped next to some dinosaur-excavations, another breathtaking place it was I have to say - I climbed the next hill again in the evening.

5th day
We got to a national park where a long strip of sandy dunes cuts the Gobi in half, with a blooming vegetation right next to it - kinda strange, but looks really gorgeous. You have to pay an entrance fee to get to the dunes, and then we payed some extra for a camel ride - riding this ancient piece of creatures gave me a feeling something like driving a Renault 4 in Tokyo... ÷) We made a couple of jackass-videos on the dunes and then checked out the prices of a local restaurant we found there, they were offering dinners for 10$, so we laughed a while in their faces and then went camping again with noodles and Mongolian soup. (Our driver was making this soup every evening out of dried meat and mushrooms, yummy!)

6th day
We made ourselves on some incredibly bumpy roads towards an other protected area called Eagle-valley. Saw a taki-horse on the way! (This is an other ancient breed, they are very rare to see and have been brought back from disappearing by nature activists.) We got in for free 'cause the area supervisor was ass drunk when we arrived, but I didn't profit much of it, I got fever in the afternoon and had to rest on my butt instead of hiking. Took some drugs, watched the eagles up in the sky, read a half book and went sleeping early.

7th day
We took a day extra in the valley, and I got somewhat better so I went to see the ice in the deep pass of the area where it stays frozen during the whole summer - in the middle of the Gobi desert. I even had strength to hike a smaller hill and take a look around, it was really beautiful again. From this point we didn't have anything else but to get back to Ulanbaatar, so we crawled up in the afternoon and made ourselves on the way.

8th day
Driving, driving, driving... In the evening we were sleeping in a "hotel", the room was furnished with 4 beds and a tap with a little holder above it which you had to refill from a big barrel outside the door. ÷)

9th day
We got back to UB before 12 a.m. and I felt really shitty so I just checked my mails and asked Miha to get some postcards for me, packed my things, wrote the travel log and went sleeping. Next morning my flight goes back to Helsinki, hight time to get back to civilisation! ÷) Miha made some pics in the evening of Ulanbaatar again, here they are:

Epilogue

Leaving Asia
I parted Miha in the morning, he decided finally to continue his trip towards Beijing, and spent indeed nearly another month there. The whole adventure took a month for me, and I had more time in trains than in my whole life before I guess. Sadly I didn't have the time to travel any further to the East, but I have the intention to do so once again, beginning with China probably, who knows?
