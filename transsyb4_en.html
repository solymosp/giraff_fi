Irkutsk

An interesting place
Irkutsk is a quite special place in Russia I guess, it used to be some sort of intellectual shelter for those thinkers who have been mentally exiled from Moscow and the main cities during the communism. Today it's developing rapidly, but houses and streets are still in poor condition, the public marketplace reminds me on some documentaries I saw about India. Loads of people have cars with steering wheel on the right - they are a blooming second-hand car market for Japan, many people travel to Vladivostok to get a 2-3 years old Japanese car for around 2-3000$ which is actually way cheaper than what a new Russian Lada would cost them. Normal 95 octane petrol costs less then half EUR/l, but taxi drivers are trying to make their fortune out of tourism - in the first turn they offer you a lift for 200 Rubel, which you can haggle down to 100 in ten seconds if you try. Though that's quite an acceptable price for a European, mind that a normal minibus ride costs 5-8 RUB. There are some nice youth hostels around to take a rest, they offer nights for 8-10 EUR. Another difference is that you can get a registration for half the money you have to pay in St. Petersburg or Moscow, so it's worth beginning the whole trip in the East and going backwards by train. Aye, and a good advice: don't drink unboiled milk, even the Tetra Pak stuff is gonna fuck up your stomach. ÷)

Interesting thing was the change in people's general attitude, they smile a lot more than in the two big cities where they're actually frowning all the time, even a joiner has enough fingers to count smiling people on a daily basis. A nice portion of the population is Buyrat, that means black haired mongoloid type - if you go into the countryside, many villages are pure Buyrats.

The Olkhon island

Out of civilisation
We got to a nice island on the lake, it took around 6 hours from Irkutsk by bus. There's nothing but a few fishermen's villages, few hours of electricity due to a nice generator, no waterpipes, no rush, no noise. The tourists group up at a place called "Nikita's homestead", eat fish every day, and have long discussions in the evening. It's just next to the most beautiful part of the Island, the "Shaman rock", you cannot find a brochure of the lake without a picture of this. Measures are amazing, it's definitely huge and with a deepness of 1.6 km it holds 20% of the globe's freshwater reserves. It's water is chrystal-clear, drinkable, and damn cold, it was some 5 degrees centigrade by the time we were there. The flora and fauna is exceptional, some 80% of the plants and animals are typical of this place and cannot be found anywhere else - if you think that biologists are easy to find around, you're certainly right. ÷)

People
We took a one day's trip to the Eastern shore, with a driver and a younger guide. From their discussion and attitude it was easy to see how differently they think, elder people still have thoughts about the good deeds of the communists, and have much less respect towards the Buyrats than the younger generation. They see them as kinsmen of Mongolians, dirty, foreign, though they have been living in Russia for a good while now. The immigration of the Mongolians happening today is another phenomenon to strengthen these feelings. We got some information on salaries as well, the young guy was working in a theather as a technician, he is getting a monthly 7000 RUB - that makes about 200 EUR.

Russians are generally very superstitious, and here they have lots of leftover beliefs from the old shamanism they had before. The Shaman Rock is not meant to be climbed by women for instance, some things bring bad luck, etcetc. Interesting thing is that they have practically the same type of sauna as Finns have, even the bouquet of birchtree-branches (the "vihta") is in use. Taking care of common goods is not exactly in practice, people just take out the trash onto the meadow, rusting cars here and there, nobody cares. I guess it's going to take some more decades to sort out these sick remainders of the past regime.

Ulan Ude

A pinch of buddhism
Getting a Mongolian visa in Irkutsk was fairly easy, costs 25 bucks with an extra 25 for the one-day express delivery. So we did it in one day and took a night-train to the other side of the lake, a town called Ulan Ude - this is already a peace of Mongolia, it has a Mongolian name (means Red River), a buddhist monastery, and lots uf Buyrats. Another interesting thing is that this place has the biggest Lenin-head monument ever!
