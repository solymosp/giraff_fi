On the way into Russia

Crossing the border
At the border everything went quite smooth, the building itself was obviously built by the Finns, typical Finnish details in Russian condition - stinky toilets. Behind the desk there was a woman somewhere in her mid 40's with an UV lamp as big as a microwave, she had her hard times to get it workin', all her equipment was huge, unpractical and pretty old. We passed without problems, and I didn't have a clue she forgot to give me some important papers...

Night view from the bus
As it was a nightbus, I didn't strictly follow the surroundings, but I very much remember a smaller town we passed with marvellous buildings originating definitely from before the communism in such a low condition that actually they were hardly standing. Fassades absolutely run down, bricks hanging out every here and there, I haven't seen the same phenomenon during the rest of my stay in the country - most of the buildings were somewhat repaired or built during the communist era.

First look
The city looked cleaner than I expected. Streets and roads are generally in quite good condition, even the cars are of higher standard than I thought - hard to find a Moskwitch or an old-type round-lamp Lada 1200. The staircases and inner yards of the buildings tend to look really messed up, they obviously have no private owners to look after them. On the contrary, many of the flats are privatized and kept in good condition after you have stumbled through the ruiny stairs. ÷) Many churches and public buildings have been renewed, the city has an immense income from tourism - isn't difficult to find a quality place for accommodation either, here's mine located in the very center right behind the Hermitage. Would you want to have quality service, rely on this hostel, I heard bad news about several hotels charging nice fees for a night, while we were fully and elegantly served during our stay at the Nordhostel!

It's a f.... small world!
As Miha went straight to Moscow, I was alone in St. Petersburg - and finding some company was easier than I thought. Right away in the hostel I met Tony and Constantine, both from the USA travelling around alone. So we grouped up and got into the city together, and as they were asking each other where they actually live, it turned out that they can easily get over to each other on foot in about 5 minutes. Newest country-ad: "Come to Russia and meet your neighbors!" ÷)

Visa problems
Being in Russia is a dead story when avoiding the maze of the local burocracy, so I had to try how things really work. When entering the country, every foreigner is supposed to get a registration card which is to be stamped in 3 days by an authorized party to prove your place of staying in Russia. I didn't have a clue about all this, and - thanks to the nice lady at the border - I didn't even get my registration card, so I was absolutely calm about my papers and legality. I got the information after 2 days, when I hastily tried to get my registration not to run into difficulties if I ever want to leave this country. Normally, hotels have the right to registrate, and they do it for a certain fee if you're not their guest (showing you up as a guest of course). I was sent away from many places including the official OVIR office because I didn't have a "registration card" while nobody took the time to explain what it is actually and how I could get one. I ended up at the consulate, where typical post-soviet officials tried to figure out this question for me phoning around the ministry of foreign affairs and so, where the very last answer I got was that I should go and bribe somebody for a stamp, that's how it works. Cool, ha? ÷) In the end, Masha (the landlady - ehm, landgirl ÷) - at the Nordhostel) was the one to know the details, and she organized me a card with a last minute stamp so that I got out of this mess - even so, it took nearly my whole day. Another option would have been to bribe the migration officer at the border to overlook my lack of papers, which is a well-working solution as well - the problem is that any time in Russia you may be checked, and police officers tend to check foreigners on the street for they know many of them have these problems, and they're always good subjects for getting a bribe.

Vodka...
... is something you cannot really avoid when being in Russia, so we made our way to the Vodka Museum. My first real Russian Vodka I had here, and I got into the history and background of this famous (and rather tasteless ÷) lever balm of Russia. The real Vodka is made of grain, is exactly 40% strong, clear transparent and tasteless (YES, it is a requirement!). The standards of the Vodka production have been established by Mendjelejev, the founder of the periodical table - no doubt the latter had a smaller response among ordinary people. ÷) With Tony and Constantine I have tasted 3 kinds of Vodka, including a basic one, the favourite of the tourist guide and the most expensive, high end Vodka having special bottles and produced in limited quantites. Can you guess our rating? (Yop, they were practically the same. Slightly less roughness in the extra type, for doubleprice of course. ÷) The rest of the day we spent with pointless laughing and somewhat uncertain steps...
